The same peninsula looks different depending whose map you open

Crimea has been administered by Russia since a 2014 annexation that most of the world doesn't recognize. Open a map of the region, and which country you're browsing from quietly decides what you see.

🌍 Viewed globally

Encircled, dashed

Most international map providers draw Crimea with a distinct dashed border — acknowledging Russia's on-the-ground control since 2014 without endorsing the annexation.

Russia annexed Crimea in March 2014, days after Russian forces took control of the peninsula and a disputed referendum was held under military occupation. The UN General Assembly responded with Resolution 68/262, affirming Ukraine's territorial integrity within its internationally recognized borders, including Crimea — and the vast majority of the world's governments still do not recognize the annexation as legitimate.

In practice, that leaves cartographers picking a side just by drawing a line. Russian state maps show no distinction at all. Ukrainian maps show no distinction either, just the opposite direction. Everyone else tends to split the difference with a dashed or dotted boundary — the standard cartographic signal for "administered by one party, claimed by another."

Sources: UN General Assembly Resolution 68/262 (2014); Russian Federal State Statistics Service regional listings; Ukrainian State Geocadastre. Country shapes: Natural Earth. Crimea boundary: geoBoundaries (Ukraine ADM1, "Autonomous Republic of Crimea" + "Sevastopol", dissolved and simplified).
